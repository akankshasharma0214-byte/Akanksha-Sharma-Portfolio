Section 03
Trend Analysis

Revenue Growth — Interest Rate Cycle Amplifies Exceptional Fundamentals. JPMorgan's net revenue grew from $119.5B in 2020 to $177.6B in 2024 — a 49% increase over four years. The 2023 step-change to $158.1B (+24%) was driven by two forces: the Federal Reserve's aggressive rate hike cycle lifting net interest income from $54B to $90B, and the opportunistic acquisition of First Republic Bank in May 2023 following its FDIC-assisted failure — adding approximately $200B in deposits and a significant wealth management client base. The underlying organic business performance — record investment banking fee market share, double-digit payments growth, and record AWM AUM — demonstrates that JPMorgan's revenue growth is not merely cyclical but reflects deepening competitive moats across all business lines.

Profitability Cycle — Credit Reserve Dynamics Distort Year-to-Year Comparisons. Net income swings between years are heavily influenced by provision for credit losses: the $17.5B COVID reserve build in 2020 suppressed earnings, the subsequent $5B+ reserve release in 2021 inflated them to $48.3B, and the $6–10B annual reserve builds in 2022–2024 reflect normalisation. Stripping out reserve movements, the underlying earning power of the franchise has grown consistently — ROTCE ex-significant items has been 18–22% throughout the period, significantly above the 12–15% peer median. The 2024 record of $58.5B net income on a reported basis confirms that the earnings power is real, not accounting-driven.

Capital Position — Fortress Strategy Strengthening. CET1 capital ratio has grown from 13.1% to 15.7% — well above the regulatory minimum of approximately 12% — reflecting disciplined earnings retention and risk-weighted asset management. The $547B Total Loss-Absorbing Capacity is the highest of any global bank. This capital strength enables JPMorgan to simultaneously absorb economic stress, fund organic growth, make opportunistic acquisitions (First Republic 2023), and return capital aggressively — $18.8B in share buybacks in 2024 alone. The overhead ratio declining from 59% to 50% over the period reflects operating leverage as revenue grows faster than expenses.

Competitive Position — Widening Moat Across All Segments. JPMorgan has been #1 in global investment banking fees for 15+ consecutive years, #1 in US retail deposits for 4 consecutive years, and maintains the largest US card loan portfolio. The combination of scale ($4T balance sheet), distribution (86M+ retail customers), technology investment ($17B+ annual technology spend), and risk management culture under Jamie Dimon creates competitive advantages that are structurally difficult to replicate. The 2025 acquisition guidance signals continued appetite for bolt-on acquisitions in payments and wealth management.

JPMorgan trades at approximately $270 per share — 2.4x tangible book value and 13.5x forward earnings. For banks, the appropriate valuation framework is Price-to-Tangible Book relative to ROTCE: a bank generating 22% ROTCE against a 10% cost of equity deserves to trade at a significant premium to book value. Our residual income model — which values banks based on the excess return over cost of equity applied to TBV — produces an intrinsic value range of $250–310 per share, suggesting the stock is approximately fairly valued with modest upside in the base case. The primary downside risk to valuation is NII compression from rate cuts which would reduce ROTCE toward 16–17% — compressing the justified P/TBV multiple from 2.4x toward 1.8–2.0x. The primary upside catalyst is IB fee recovery and M&A market normalisation demonstrating durable non-NII revenue growth.

Section 08
FP&A Analyst — Executive Takeaway
For the CFO / Board of Directors

JPMorgan Chase delivered a record financial performance in 2024 — $58.5B in net income, 22% ROTCE, and a 15.7% CET1 ratio — that represents the culmination of a decade of disciplined investment in franchise strength under Jamie Dimon's leadership. The core financial challenge for 2025–2027 is managing the transition from peak Net Interest Income ($94B in 2024) toward a normalised rate environment while demonstrating that non-NII revenue streams — investment banking, payments, asset management, and private credit — can fill the earnings gap. The Board's primary financial focus should be on the NII normalisation trajectory and its implications for the sustainable EPS run rate. JPMorgan's competitive advantages — #1 global IB, #1 US retail deposits, $3.9T AUM, and the deepest technology moat in banking at $17B+ annual investment — provide a uniquely diversified earnings base that differentiates the firm from interest-rate-sensitive peers. The key question is not whether JPMorgan will remain the world's most profitable bank — it almost certainly will — but at what EPS level earnings stabilise as rates normalise.
